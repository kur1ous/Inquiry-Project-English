Fairness

If moral sense is entirely taught, it should not appear until after socialization. However, infants demonstrate moral evaluation before they can speak or even walk.

The Baby Lab: Yale Infant Cognition Center

Psychologist Karen Wynn and her team at Yale developed ingenious experiments to test moral cognition in pre-verbal infants. Because babies cannot speak, researchers measure where they look and what they reach for.

The Helper/Hinderer Study

Infants as young as 3 months watch a puppet show. A circle tries to climb a hill. A "helper" triangle pushes it up. A "hinderer" square pushes it down.

When offered both puppets, over 80% of infants reach for the helper. They prefer the morally "good" character before they have been taught anything about morality.

Key insight: This preference appears before language, before walking, before any possibility of explicit moral instruction.

Fairness Expectations

By 15 months, infants expect resources to be divided fairly. When shown two recipients receiving unequal shares, they look longer, indicating surprise at the violation of expected fairness.

Key insight: Infants do not merely prefer helpful agents; they have expectations about distributive justice. Fairness is not just learned; it is expected.

Beyond Humans: Primate Fairness

If moral intuitions evolved, we might expect to find precursors in our closest relatives. The evidence is striking.

The Grape and Cucumber Experiment

Primatologist Frans de Waal trained capuchin monkeys to perform a simple task for a food reward. When both monkeys received cucumber, they were content. But when one monkey received a grape (a higher-value reward) while the other received cucumber for the same work, the disadvantaged monkey refused to participate.

They would reject the cucumber entirely, sometimes throwing it at the researcher. The monkeys were not just hungry; they were indignant.

"If two individuals perform the same task, they expect the same reward. And if there's a difference, they become very upset."

— Frans de Waal, primatologist

This "inequity aversion" has been documented in chimpanzees, bonobos, dogs, and corvids (crows). It suggests that fairness intuitions predate humanity by millions of years.

What This Suggests

This evidence suggests that basic moral intuitions are innate. They appear too early in human development to be taught, and they appear across species that share no culture with us.

Evolution seems to have selected for moral intuitions because they enable cooperation. Fairness and helping are not arbitrary social constructs, they are adaptations that solved problems of group living.

While the capacity for moral intuition appears innate, the content remains malleable. Infants prefer helpers, but which groups count as worthy of help is learned. The foundation is biological; the architecture is cultural.

Reflection Point

The infant research shows that babies prefer helpful characters before they can be taught. Does this evidence shift your slider toward "innate"? Why or why not?
