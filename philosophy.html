Philosophical Counterpoints

Before modern science, philosophers grappled with the same question. Their frameworks help organize and challenge the empirical evidence.

The Classical Positions

John Locke: Tabula Rasa (1689)

Locke argued that the mind at birth is a "blank slate" (tabula rasa). All knowledge, including moral knowledge, comes from experience. There are no innate ideas, no instinctual moral sense.

"Let us suppose the mind to be, as we say, white paper, void of all characters, without any ideas: How comes it to be furnished? ... To this I answer, in one word, from experience."

— John Locke, An Essay Concerning Human Understanding

Implication: Conscience is entirely constructed. A child raised in a society that valued cruelty would develop a "conscience" that approved of cruelty.

Jean-Jacques Rousseau: Natural Goodness (1762)

Rousseau countered that humans are born with an innate moral sense, a natural sympathy and aversion to suffering. Society does not create morality; it corrupts it.

"Man is naturally good, and only by institutions is he made bad."

— Jean-Jacques Rousseau, Émile

Implication: Conscience is innate; socialization risks distorting our natural moral instincts rather than creating them.

Immanuel Kant: Rational Morality (1785)

Kant proposed that morality is neither instinctual feeling nor learned behavior, but a product of pure reason. Any rational being would derive the same moral laws through logic alone.

"Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law."

— Immanuel Kant, Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals

Implication: Conscience is neither biological nor social but rational. It is innate in the sense that reason is innate, but it must be developed through education.

Evolutionary Ethics

Contemporary philosophers and scientists have attempted to integrate these perspectives with evolutionary biology.

The Evolutionary Account

Moral intuitions evolved because they solved problems of social living. Cooperation, fairness, and empathy enabled our ancestors to form stable groups, a massive survival advantage.

On this view:

  • Locke was wrong: we are not blank slates. We have evolved moral instincts.
  • Rousseau was partly right: natural sympathy has biological roots.
  • Kant was partly right: moral reasoning builds on instinct but extends beyond it.

Unresolved Conflicts

The evidence you've encountered creates philosophical conflicts that resist easy resolution:

Hardware vs. Software

The brain provides moral hardware (neural triad, infant fairness). But the software, (moral priorities), is clearly learned. Where does biology end and culture begin?

Flexibility vs. Fragility

Conscience can be shaped by culture (Romanian orphans, residential schools) and broken by physiology (Minnesota Experiment, brain damage). Is something so malleable truly "innate"?

Universality vs. Variation

Some moral intuitions (fairness, empathy) appear cross-culturally. Yet cultures vary tremendously in who deserves moral consideration and what counts as harm. How do we reconcile universal foundations with variable expression?

Final Reflection
