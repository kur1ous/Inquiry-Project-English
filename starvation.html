The Minnesota Starvation Experiment

What happens to conscience when the body is systematically deprived?

The Study

In 1944, physiologist Ancel Keys recruited 36 conscientious subjects, young men who had refused combat on moral grounds, for a year-long study on starvation and recovery. These were not ordinary subjects; they were selected specifically for their strong moral convictions.

The Protocol

For six months, participants were restricted to approximately 1,560 calories per day, about half their normal intake. They were required to walk 22 miles per week and perform various tasks.

The goal was to simulate conditions faced by famine victims in war-torn Europe and develop protocols for rehabilitation.

Physical Effects

Participants lost approximately 25% of their body weight. They experienced chronic weakness, dizziness, edema, and heart problems. Their metabolisms slowed dramatically as their bodies fought to conserve energy.

The Psychological Collapse

Loss of Social Interest

Men who had been warm and engaged became withdrawn, irritable, and self-obsessed. Conversations narrowed to food. Friendships dissolved. Several participants reported that they simply stopped caring about others.

Moral Deterioration

Behaviors that would have been unthinkable before the study became common:

  • Cheating on the diet, then lying about it
  • Hoarding food and eating in secret
  • Fantasies of violence (one participant dreamed of cannibalism)
  • Complete abandonment of previous values and commitments

One man, overcome with guilt about eating food scraps, cut off three of his own fingers. The moral agony of violating their principles coexisted with the inability to resist.

Recovery and Aftermath

During the recovery phase, as adequate nutrition returned, so did the men's moral characters. Their empathy returned. Their irritability subsided. They became recognizably themselves again.

But the experience left permanent marks. Many participants spent the rest of their lives unable to waste food. Several dedicated their careers to hunger relief, haunted by what they had learned about human fragility.

What This Suggests

The Minnesota Experiment demonstrates that conscience has physiological preconditions. Moral behavior is not a disembodied act of will, it requires a brain adequately fueled and functioning.

This supports a materialist view of conscience: moral cognition, like all cognition, depends on biological infrastructure. Damage that infrastructure, whether through lesions, trauma, or starvation, and moral capacity degrades.

Under sufficiently extreme conditions, almost anyone can be reduced to moral collapse. The participants were selected for their moral strength, and it was not enough.

Recovery of moral character tracked recovery of nutrition. The men were not permanently broken. This suggests the moral damage was to their temporarily impaired biological substrate.

Reflection Point

At what point in the starvation process did participants describe themselves as "losing themselves"? What does this suggest about the relationship between body and conscience?
